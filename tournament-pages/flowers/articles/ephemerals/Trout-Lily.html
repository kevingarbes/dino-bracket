Trout Lily

Trout lily (Erythronium americanum) is named for the mottled pattern on its leaves, which resembles brook trout skin. This little lily pops up in rich woods and streamsides, showing one nodding yellow flower per plant. A colony of trout lilies can be extremely long-lived—the patch slowly spreads by underground bulbs, and some colonies in the Eastern U.S. are estimated to be over 100 years old. Despite their brief bloom time (a week or two in early spring), they are important early nectar sources. Fun fact: trout lilies often take several years to mature from seed to flowering, so a blooming colony is a sign of an undisturbed, older forest.
