Pasque Flower

The pasque flower (Pulsatilla patens), also called prairie crocus, is a silky, lavender-purple wildflower that blooms on the plains and prairies often around Easter (hence “pasque,” meaning Easter). It’s the state flower of South Dakota and a welcome sight soon after the snow melts. Covered in fine hairs, the entire plant is adapted to cold - the hairy coating provides warmth and protection from spring frosts. Indigenous Lakota people called it “hosh kecampte” (meaning “smells like a buffalo”), and indeed the pasque flower has a musky scent to attract early spring bees. By summer it forms fluffy seed heads and then disappears, waiting through the hot season and winter to rise again at the next paschal time.
