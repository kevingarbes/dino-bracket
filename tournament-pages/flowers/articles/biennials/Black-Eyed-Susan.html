Black-Eyed Susan

Black-eyed Susan (Rudbeckia hirta) is usually grown as an easy annual or short-lived perennial, but in the wild it often behaves as a biennial—rosettes the first year, golden blooms the next. As Maryland’s state flower, its golden petals and dark center “eye” are iconic in summer meadows. The name comes from an Old English poem by John Gay, in which the flower symbolizes a maiden (Susan) reunited with her lover (Sweet William), forever linking those two flower names in horticulture lore.
